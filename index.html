Finding the Flight of
Least Resistance
by: Zoe Exelbert

The Great Salt Lake is a prominent stopping point for over 10 million birds year-round. Situated on the Pacific Flyway Route, the Great Salt Lake is a top attraction to over 338 species who utilize the lake's resources for breeding, feeding, and temporary habitats during seasonal migration periods.

The flight to the Great Salt Lake for these birds has never been questioned, as it is an evolved response to the Great Salt Lake's continuous offering of food and habitat safety. However, as the climate begins to warm, water levels are dropping and food is becoming more scarce.

Two species in specific, the Eared Grebe and the American White Pelican, are extremely vulnerable to the environmental deterioration of the lake. With the majority of their continental population coming to the GSL every year, if conditions continue to worsen, both of these species are in danger.

Here we will investigate the climate effects these birds are vulnerable to as we fly along with them over the years, answering the question, is flying to the Great Salt lake still the flight of least resistance?

The Eared Grebe, the most abundant grebe species in the world, begins their flight South from the Pacific Northwest towards the Great Salt Lake around September.

During this time in the Fall, Grebes come to stock up on food before heading to Mexico for the winter. Then, in the Spring, the Grebes will come back to breed and nest at the GSL.
